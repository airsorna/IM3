    Convention and Context by Steven Mailloux discusses how conventions are dependent on context. He makes a valid point when he says, "We can discuss conventions as ways of acting or as accomplishments of meaning, not only in literary and critical texts but also in discourses circulating in other cultural and social spheres." (Mailloux,1983:409). What I understand this to mean is that conventions are not a set of rules that must be followed at all times, but rather that conventions work hand in hand with context. In any given context, there are a set of conventions to be followed. How do I relate this to my understanding of UI and UX? Similar to conventions, UI and UX have a set of conventions one has to follow when designing a website, but again, not everything will apply as context is important. Maybe the website you plan to create doesn’t have an "about" page, for example. This is why context is important.

    Reading Reflection

    User experience - a research agenda by Marc Hassenzahl and Noam Tractinsky offers a comprehensive framework for user interface design, providing three prominent perspectives for understanding user interfaces. These include Beyond the instrumental, which speaks to the holistic and aesthetic nature of UI; emotion and affect, which speak to the subjective, positive, and antecedent nature of UI; and the experimental, which is about the complex and dynamic nature of UI. The combination of these three elements forms what we know as the user interface.

    A close reading of Graham et al’s "Toward a study of information geographies".

    Summary

    The article seeks to understand how information is distributed across the world. The study examines the factors and contributors of information distribution in the internet era. "Information has always had geography. It is from somewhere; about somewhere; it evolves and is transformed somewhere; it is mediated by networks, infrastructures, and technologies, all of which exist in physical, material places. " (Graham et al., 2015). In summary, information originates from, engages with, and is adapted to a variety of local contexts and geographies, ranging from conventional measures to instruction manuals to myths and legends. This essay explores a variety of contemporary information geographies to gain access to patterns of mutability, mobility, and underlying power dynamics in connection to historical arrangements in the production and use of information.

    What is Information Geography?

    Information is defined as "knowledge obtained from investigation, study, or instruction" (Merriam-Webster, 2022) and geography as "Geography is the study of places and the relationships between people and their environments." (National Geographic, 2022). So, therefore, information geography is knowledge obtained from investigation, study, or instruction of places and the relationships between people and their environments.

    Reflect critically on their conclusions.

    Information has always been spatial in nature. It is made someplace, it is utilized elsewhere, and it travels between locations (Graham et al., 2015). And information geography has always been entangled with power dynamics. Certain people have significantly more influence than others, and some locations are vital in information ecosystems while others are peripheral.

    Many, however, have turned to the internet as a means of overcoming some of these old limits. In principle, access to the internet allows users to access the sum total of all codified human knowledge; it allows everyone to compete on a more equal playing field. This is due to the fact that there are minimal geographical barriers to information transmission through the internet. With a few exceptions (particularly China and a few other totalitarian countries), content such as a Wikipedia entry or a Google Book is available to anybody on the planet. However, it is important to recognize that the internet, and the information contained within it, are also characterized by actual spatial disparities. The vast majority of people have never used the internet, and certain parts of the world have very little representation in our digital world.

    Reflection on Traveling While Trans by Costanza-Chock

    Sasha Costanza-Chock begins the reading by narrating an incident that occurred to her at the airport security checkpoint. As a member of the LGBTQ community, Costanza-Chock doesn't conform to the gender norms society has established. She identifies as a transgender woman. This therefore becomes an issue when passing through body scanners at the airport because the machines are only programmed to recognize two genders (male and female), so as a result, people like Costanza-Chock struggle because "humans have trained our machines to categorize faces and bodies as male and female through lenses tinted by the optics of white supremacy" (Costanza-Chock, 2020: p. 4-5). This then calls for a new approach to making technology, as the world has evolved and our understanding of human life has too.

    According to Sasha Costanza-Chock, "design justice" is an approach to design that is led by marginalized communities and that aims explicitly to challenge, rather than reproduce, structural inequalities. It has emerged from a growing community of designers in various fields who work closely with social movements and community-based organizations around the world." (Costanza-Chock, 2020). It aspires to equal treatment of all people in technology and information, regardless of skin color, ability, gender, age, personal circumstances, or societal context. It presents itself as a solution to white supremacist heteropatriarchy, ableism, capitalism, and settler colonialism through user interface and user experience design by deploying good user-centered design so that "we use design to sustain, heal, and empower our communities, as well as to seek liberation from exploitative and oppressive systems" (Neil, 2021). Design justice is one of the ways inequality and discrimination are being addressed and resolved within the global internet community.

    Society

    The Cambridge Dictionary defines society as "a large group of people who live together in an organized manner, making decisions about how to do things and sharing the work that needs to be done." (Cambridge Dictionary, 2022). A gathering of people in one organized place can be considered a society, so Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter can be described as a digital society. "The notion of digital society reflects the results of the modern society in adopting and integrating information and communication technologies at home, work, education and recreation." (LIBRe, 2022). Participation in the digital society is virtual for the purposes of representation, as we know that societies construct patterns of behavior by deeming certain actions or concepts as acceptable or unacceptable. These patterns of behavior within a given society are known as societal norms. It is important that everyone is a part of the conversation and voices their concerns about the injustices of any society. In closing, "free internet access should be indispensable to all members of an organized society." (Kasapis, 2020). for the betterment of all who live, not just a select few.
